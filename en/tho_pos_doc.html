The Intellectual Position

The organisation doesn't copy the leader's strategy. It copies the leader's nervous system.

This is not a metaphor. Barsade's research on emotional contagion (2002) demonstrated that a leader's affective state transfers to teams within milliseconds — below the threshold of conscious awareness. Keltner's Power Paradox research (2016) confirmed that power systematically erodes the empathy required to notice this is happening. The result: every unresolved personal story a leader carries becomes, by neurobiological default, the operating system of the entire organisation.

Porges' Polyvagal Theory names this precisely: the nervous system assesses safety or threat before any cognitive processing occurs. When leadership signals threat — through silence, political caution, or performed confidence — every person in the system responds as if the threat is real. That response shapes communication, decision-making, creativity, and trust. Not because people choose it. Because the body does.

"Most organisations perform the company they wish they were — not the one they actually are. The gap between the two is where talent exits, innovation stalls, and partnerships quietly erode."
Tom Waldek

Frankl's contribution — often overlooked in business — completes the picture: survival patterns persist not from weakness but from meaning absence. When a leader loses access to their own WHY, performance fills the void. That performance then becomes the culture. This is the origin of every pattern The Human Origin is built to surface.

The Methodology

Six film disciplines applied to organisational neurobiology.

The Human Origin draws on six professional film disciplines — not as metaphor, but as operational frameworks. Each was developed by practitioners whose work required accessing what is real in human beings under pressure, in high-stakes environments, within systems of hierarchy and performance. That is precisely the context organisations operate in. Each discipline is applied in real meetings, real decisions, real conversations — in the room where it matters.

01 — Writing
Surfacing the narrative beneath the performance.
In screenwriting, character is built from personal story. This discipline identifies the narratives leaders and teams carry below the surface — and how those narratives drive behaviour.
02 — Directing
Creating conditions where truth shows up.
A director never tells actors what to feel. They adjust the environment until what's real has no choice but to appear. Applied in real meetings, real decisions.
03 — Acting
The difference between performed safety and real presence.
An actor's real craft: accessing what's true and acting from there. Replaces the version pressure taught everyone to perform with something genuine.
04 — Editing
Cutting what no longer serves the story.
Every film has a kill-your-darlings moment. Organisations do too: the rituals, hierarchies, and unspoken rules that keep people hiding rather than serving the work.
05 — Producing
Turning creative vision into operational reality.
A producer builds the backbone that lets everyone do their best creative work — support, communication, protection. The structure that holds people so they can stop holding it together alone.
06 — Intimacy Coordination
The architecture where vulnerability is safe.
Film's newest and most revolutionary discipline. The conditions where anyone — leaders, teams, partners — can say "I don't know" without it costing them safety or belonging.
